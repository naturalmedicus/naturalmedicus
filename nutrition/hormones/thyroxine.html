What is Thyroxine (T4)?

Thyroxine, also known as T4, is a hormone produced by the thyroid gland in the human body. It is one of two primary hormones produced by the thyroid gland, the other being triiodothyronine (T3). Thyroxine plays a vital role in regulating metabolism and the body's energy levels.

Thyroxine is synthesized from iodine and the amino acid tyrosine, and is released into the bloodstream, where it circulates throughout the body and interacts with cells to regulate their metabolism. The levels of thyroxine in the body are regulated by the hypothalamus and pituitary gland, which monitor the levels of thyroid hormones in the blood and adjust the thyroid's production of hormones as needed.

Thyroxine levels can have a significant impact on overall health, and imbalances in thyroxine levels can lead to conditions such as hypothyroidism (too little thyroxine) or hyperthyroidism (too much thyroxine).

Thyroxine (T4) binds to specific receptors known as thyroid hormone receptors (TRs) located in the nucleus of target cells. These receptors are members of the nuclear receptor superfamily, which includes other hormone receptors such as those for estrogen, androgens, and glucocorticoids.

When thyroxine binds to a thyroid hormone receptor, it initiates a cascade of intracellular events that lead to changes in gene expression and ultimately affect cellular metabolism. The actions of thyroxine and its receptor are complex and affect a wide range of physiological processes throughout the body.

Thyroxine can also be converted into the more biologically active form of thyroid hormone, triiodothyronine (T3), which also binds to thyroid hormone receptors and has similar effects on cellular metabolism. [R]

There are thyroxine antagonists, also known as antithyroid drugs, which are used to treat hyperthyroidism (an overactive thyroid gland) by reducing the production of thyroid hormones, including thyroxine (T4). The two main types of antithyroid drugs are:

PTU works by inhibiting the activity of an enzyme called thyroperoxidase, which is involved in the synthesis of thyroid hormones. This results in a decrease in the production of both T4 and triiodothyronine (T3). [R]

MMI works by inhibiting the activity of an enzyme called thyroperoxidase, similar to PTU. However, MMI is more selective and specific for inhibiting T4 production, while having less effect on T3 production. [R]

Both PTU and MMI can be used to treat hyperthyroidism, but they work in slightly different ways and may have different side effects. These drugs are usually used in combination with other treatments for hyperthyroidism, such as beta blockers or radioactive iodine therapy.

Some women may experience changes in thyroid function during pregnancy or postpartum period, which can affect thyroid hormone levels and may require medical monitoring and treatment. [R]
