What is Dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA)?

DHEA stands for dehydroepiandrosterone, which is a hormone produced by the adrenal glands. It serves as a precursor to other hormones, including testosterone and estrogen. DHEA is classified as a steroid hormone and plays various roles in the body.

While dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA) itself does not have specific receptors, it can exert its effects through conversion into other hormones that do have receptors.

DHEA is converted into androgens (such as testosterone) and estrogens (such as estradiol) in peripheral tissues. These androgens and estrogens, in turn, bind to their respective receptors in the body to produce various physiological effects. For example, androgens can bind to androgen receptors in target tissues, while estrogens can bind to estrogen receptors.

The effects of DHEA are therefore mediated indirectly through its metabolites, which interact with specific hormone receptors. The exact mechanisms of how DHEA and its metabolites exert their effects are still being studied, and the physiological roles of DHEA in the body are not yet fully understood.

There can be several causes of low DHEA levels. Some possible causes include:

  • Aging
  • Adrenal insufficiency
  • Chronic stress
  • Certain medical conditions
  • Medications or treatments

DHEA levels naturally decline with age. As individuals get older, the production of DHEA by the adrenal glands decreases, leading to lower circulating levels.

Conditions that affect the adrenal glands, such as adrenal insufficiency or Addison's disease, can result in decreased production of DHEA. These conditions disrupt the normal functioning of the adrenal glands, leading to insufficient hormone production, including DHEA.

Prolonged or chronic stress can lead to dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, which controls the production of various hormones, including DHEA. Excessive stress can result in increased cortisol production at the expense of DHEA production.

Some medical conditions, such as hypopituitarism or congenital adrenal hyperplasia, can affect hormone production and lead to low DHEA levels.

Certain medications, such as glucocorticoids (corticosteroids), can suppress adrenal function and reduce DHEA production. Additionally, treatments such as radiation therapy or surgical removal of the adrenal glands can also result in low DHEA levels.
