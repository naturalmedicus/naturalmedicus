What is Dopamine?

Dopamine also known as 3,4 Dihydroxyphenethylamine (DA) is an excitatory neurotransmitter and a neuromodulator. Dopamine is categorized in the monoamine group of neurotransmitters with the others being norepinephrine (noradrenaline), epinephrine (adrenaline) and acetylcholine.

Dopamine, norepinephrine, and epinephrine are monoamine neurotransmitters, also categorised as catecholamines. These neurotransmitters are released in minute amounts from the brain and peripheral nervous system. They play an important role in arousal, emotion, and cognition.

Dopamine is similar to acetylcholine which is another monoamine neurotransmitter and also needed for memory, learning, attention, cognition, motivation and arousal. Any impairment in these functions can also be a sign of low dopamine instead of acetylcholine which can likely be determined through directly raising levels of either dopamine or acetylcholine. Only small amounts of acetylcholine is needed to activate dopamine. [R]

Dopamine is known as the pleasure neurotransmitter as it increases feelings of pleasure and involved in reward and punishment activity in the brain. However it has many other functions and interactions within the body. Much like acetylcholine which is needed in small amounts to activate the release of dopamine, this neurotransmitter is involved in movement and motivation. It is also involved in addiction as people repeat behaviours that stimulate dopamine release and usually triggered by pleasurable activities which includes food, sex, and drugs.

This dopamine release is mostly found in areas such as the nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex.

The adrenals are important glands involved in producing dopamine and also other neurotransmitters that act like hormones such as epinephrine (adrenaline).

Neurons generate either excitatory or inhibitory nerve impulses with some neurotransmitters being able to function as both excitatory and inhibitory.

In the brain dopamine is produced in the substantia nigra, ventral tegmental area, and hypothalamus.

Dopamine is also produced in the gastrointestinal tract. Approximately fifty per cent of dopamine is synthesised in the gastrointestinal tract by enteric neurons and intestinal epithelial cells which contributes to higher level of dopamine in hepatic portal vein. The hepatic portal vein is a blood vessel that carries blood from the gastrointestinal tract, gallbladder, pancreas and spleen to the liver.

Within the brain dopamine is produced in the substantia nigra and the ventral tegmental area. Dopamine is released by the hypothalamus. [R]

Dopamine can be found throughout the body which includes the brain, immune system, kidneys and pancreas.

L-DOPA which is the precursor for dopamine is synthesized in the brain and kidneys

Dopamine is unable to cross the blood-brain barrier and dopamine synthesis occurs in the area it is being used such as the brain, kidneys and other organs and systems.

The production of dopamine and its functions in peripheral areas does not depend on the production and functions of dopamine within the brain.

Dopamine functions in different parts of the peripheral nervous system. It has a specific function depending on the location of dopamine synthesis which can be found in blood vessels, kidneys, pancreas, digestive system and immune system.

Dopamine has 5 types of receptors and when activated each receptor has a specific function. These receptors are known as D1, D2, D3, D4 and D5. [R]

D1 Receptors are the most abundant dopamine receptor in the central nervous system which ‘prepare’ the set of possible responses with the D2 receptors then activated to ‘shape’ and ‘select’ the final response from this initial response set framework.

Not only is the dopamine neurotransmitter involved in movement, learning, attention, sleep, mood, emotions but also plays a role in pain processing as shown in studies on D2 receptors and its involvement with pain. [R]

Dopamine is one of the main neurotransmitters in our reward system and reinforces activities that increase feelings of joy which increases the feeling of wanting more of what feels good.

Dopamine plays an important role in the dopaminergic pathway (also known as the mesolimbic pathway or reward pathway) and influences the reward and punishment activity within the brain.

Dopamine plays an important role in many bodily functions such as increased heart rate and blood pressure control. Dopamine release helps increase focus levels and motivation. [R]

Dopamine can provide bursts of energy and is involved with glutamate release another excitatory neurotransmitter essential for production of inhibitory neurotransmitter GABA.

Dopamine and norepinephrine release in the frontal cortex as well as acetylcholine, another neurotransmitter which is involved in release of dopamine.

Health Benefits of Dopamine

Supports movement and coordination

Dopamine is needed for the control of motor function and voluntary movement. Like acetylcholine it helps regulate coordination and muscle movements.

Deficiency is linked to various conditions that affect movement such as Parkinson’s disease which is characterized by tremors. [R]

Increases motivation

Dopamine is the main neurotransmitter involved in motivation and increases the drive to seek rewards and achieve goals. This neurotransmitter is associated with achievement and motivational salience.

Dopamine can help with making the effort needed to be successful through its role in reward and achievement. A deficiency of dopamine is linked to procrastination.

Supports cognitive functions

Dopamine is needed for various cognitive functions being produced in the substantia nigra and ventral tegmental regions of the brain.

It plays an important role in cognitive functions such as working memory, learning, problem solving and control of attention.

Dopamine in the prefrontal cortex modulates cognitive control which influences memory. Dopamine is involved in the integration of thoughts and emotions.

Dopamine controls the flow of information from other areas of the brain which is needed for problem solving. In the cerebral cortex the frontal lobe contains the highest amounts of dopamine neurons. Dopamine limits and selects sensory information arriving from the thalamus to the forebrain.

High dopamine levels increases alertness, creativity, long-term memory and concentration which improves ability to focus and learn.

Changes in dopamine neurotransmission is implicated in cognitive control with too little causing decline in cognitive functions and too much causing racing thoughts and issues with impulse control. [R] [R] [R] [R]

Improves mood

Dopamine can boost mood in a variety of situations whether it is in relationships or partaking in an enjoyable activity.

Dopamine is involved in pleasurable reward and this can be seen when a person finds a pleasurable activity and register this to repeat in the future for the same feeling of pleasure. Meditation is associated to the blissful state and can increase dopamine levels which may help balance mood.

Dopamine is also involved in the neurochemistry of feeling love and attachment.

People in love have an increase in dopamine and this release of dopamine can increase feelings of attachment as couples feel happy to be in the presence of their partner which again plays on the reward pleasure system.

Dopamine, is one of the four main neurotransmitters that is responsible for the feelings of wellbeing and happiness. The other neurotransmitters include serotonin, oxytocin and endorphins.

These are all needed in a specific balance for these benefits on mental wellbeing and can be increased naturally through healthy diets, adequate exercise and partaking in fun activities such as dance, music and other enjoyable activities.

Low or insufficient levels of dopamine and its precursor phenylalanine and tyrosine has shown to cause an increase in anxiety levels and low mood which can lead to depression.

Dopamine is also needed for the synthesis of norepinephrine which helps improve symptoms of depression.

The D1 receptor help mediate some behavioural responses. Dopamine D1 and D2 receptor mechanisms are involved in mediating anxiety.

Supports sleep wake cycle

Dopamine is involved in the circadian rhythm from its actions in the pineal gland which regulates the sleep wake cycle. Dopamine is needed for wakefulness and involved in concentration and focus that is needed throughout the day.

Although dopamine functions are excitatory it also plays a role in sleep. Dopamine is the precursor for norepinephrine which is needed for the synthesis and release of melatonin in the pineal gland during sleep. [R]

In circadian rhythms dopamine is highest at around 7am and has shown to decrease throughout the day and decreases to the lowest level by 3 pm.

The amino acid phenylalanine is also one of the ultraviolet light absorbing amino acids needed for dopamine synthesis.

Beta brainwaves help release dopamine and this brain wave pattern is present when we are alert, awake and actively focused.

Some studies have shown that lack of sleep can decrease certain dopamine receptors however others are showing an increase of dopamine in certain brain areas implying that lack of sleep can cause an imbalance of dopamine. [R]

Involved in positive behaviour

Dopamine increases a sense of altruism which is positive in a social situation or within the community.

Dopamine is linked to altruism where a person will help others out of a selfless sense of care without any hidden motives. Altruistic people do however receive the benefit of a release of dopamine and other feel good neurotransmitter and neurohormones such as oxytocin and serotonin. Those with a sense of altruism are often accompanied by a release of dopamine after having met a need. It feels good to make others feel good.

Dopamine is implicated in social behaviour. Optimal dopamine levels reduces anxiety, increases wellbeing and allows a person to cope in stressful situations. [R]

Supports hormones

Dopamine interacts with the hormone system and has shown to increase when hormones such as estrogen and testosterone also increase. It plays a role in the brains of both men and women.

The hypothalamus gland regulates the release of hormones and is also a part of the brain where dopamine is produced. Hormones are transported to the pituitary gland and then to the different organs of the body.

Dopamine levels are increased by estradiol, a form of the dominant female hormone estrogen observed in the pre-ovulatory phase of the female monthly cycle (menstrual cycle) when estrogen levels peak [R]

Other studies on rats has also shown an association of dopamine increase with estrogen. Estrogen has also shown to play a role in cognition and may be why some women have better cognitive abilities with the rise of estrogen at specific phases of the menstrual cycle

Supports release of other neurotransmitters

Virtually all neurotransmitters discovered work in concert with each other, all of which are required in adequate amount for various important functions throughout the brain and body. Too much or too little can cause an excess or insufficient levels of other neurotransmitters.

Dopamine follows the catecholamine pathway and is needed for the synthesis of other neurotransmitters following same pathway which include norepinephrine and epinephrine.

Although dopamine converts to norepinephrine, when dopamine is the dominant neurotransmitter it inhibits release of norepinephrine which is linked to stressful situations and activates the fight or flight function. When norepinephrine is suppressed, the dopamine in blood vessels acts as a vasodilator which helps with relaxation.

D1 receptors regulate neuronal growth and development which are important for neurotransmission.

Involved in appetite and thirst

Dopamine is involved in appetite and appetite control and having the right level is important for healthy bodily functions.

Dopamine functions in the pancreas by reducing insulin production which increases food intake.

When insulin levels reduce, the blood glucose cannot enter the cells to produce energy and as a result this can increase food intake particularly high carbohydrate foods and may impact serotonin levels.

Reduced levels of dopamine increases hunger with cravings associated with tyrosine rich food such as cheese and chocolate. Serotonin can suppress hunger.

Dopamine increases ghrelin which increases hunger. [R]

In the kidneys dopamine functions include increasing sodium and urine excretion. Some studies on mice have shown that thirst and the reward associated when the thirst is quenched with either plain water or saline water is associated with the release of dopamine and is likely to be connected to dopamine’s functions in the kidneys.

Needed for Digestive health

Dopamine is important for the digestive system where most of it is produced in the gastrointestinal tract. Dopamine reduces gastrointestinal motility, protects intestinal mucosa and protects against peptic ulcer (gastroduodenal ulcer disease). [R] [R] [R]

Supports the immune system

Dopamine in the immune system is linked to reduced activity of lymphocytes. Dopamine is able to decrease inflammation and can help support the immune system in this way.

The dopaminergic system is involved in autoimmune diseases. [R]

Small amounts of alcohol can increase dopamine and it’s rewarding effects which in turn can pave the way for addiction.

Replenishing dopamine levels and keeping the catecholamines pathway strong as well as having nutrients that support MAO production which help to deactivate these neurotransmitters so that too much of it is not circulating may help benefit all kinds of addictive behaviour. Some rehabilitation programs may use tyrosine to help people with drug addiction.

Supports lactation

The tuberoinfundibular pathway which is a dopamine pathway in the brain supports breast milk production which is important for nursing mothers breastfeeding. The dopamine release in this pathway regulates release of the hormone prolactin by pituitary gland which stimulates the production of breast milk. However, prolactin rises when dopamine levels are reduced whilst higher levels of dopamine will inhibit the release of prolactin. [R]

Food Sources of Dopamine

A diet with adequate protein and other essential nutrients are needed to help maintain dopamine levels. It is important to get sufficient levels of the amino acid phenylalanine and tyrosine.

Phenylalanine can be found in most high protein food and some plant based food source. It is more abundant in animal based food than in plant based food source. Animal based food source include beef, chicken, fish (herring), dairy products such as cheese, egg, cottage cheese and chocolate. Plant based food source include almonds, peanuts apple, avocado, baked beans, soya bean, soy proteins, banana beet, carrot, parsley, pineapple, spinach, tomato.

Mammalian breast milk contains phenylalanine.

Tyrosine which is derived from phenylalanine can also be found in food. Animal protein contain the highest amount of tyrosine.

Animal food source of tyrosine are similar to phenylalanine source and includes beef, pork, turkey, duck, chicken, fish, eggs, yoghurt, cottage cheese, cheese and milk.

Wild meat such as game and venison contain higher amounts of tyrosine.

Plant based tyrosine food source includes almonds, pumpkin seeds, sesame seeds, peanuts, soya bean, soy proteins, soya products, chocolate, apples, apricot, asparagus, avocado, baked beans, legumes, capsicum, carrots, spinach, alfalfa, beet, lettuce, parsley, watercress, leek, cucumber, banana, strawberries, cherries, fig and watermelon.

There is too little tyrosine in most plant based foods such as cereals, grains, fruits and vegetables.

Phenylethylamine can be found in chocolate and cheese but is unable to reach the brain due to breaking up quickly through digestion.

Aspartame contains phenylalanine. Aspartame is an artificial sweetener found as replacement to sugar in some sugar free sodas and foods as well as some medications. However this may not be a beneficial source of phenylalanine as various studies have linked high amounts to certain health problems.

It is important to eat high protein foods to ensure adequate dopamine production.

Eating a diet with adequate cofactor nutrients is also necessary to support dopamine synthesis.

Other foods that may increase dopamine include green leafy vegetables, green tea, lima beans, oatmeal and wheatgerm.

To keep the catecholamines pathway strong and healthy it is also important to get sufficient levels of methionine.

Food intake of iron and zinc is also needed to help with synthesis of dopamine.

Food intake of vitamin B3, vitamin B6, vitamin B9, B-complex and vitamin C are water soluble and must be replenished daily.

Recommended Daily Allowance

There are no set amounts of recommended neurotransmitters. Neurotransmitter synthesis depend on the nutrients obtained from the diet or supplementation. Brainwave frequencies and other practices such as meditation can also effect levels of neurotransmitters.

Neurotransmitters are made from protein and amino acids. They also need other cofactors such as vitamins and minerals for synthesis.

Dietary protein is therefore essential and getting adequate, sufficient supply of all nutrients in the correct balance is essential.

A healthy adult needs between 40 and 70 grams of daily protein intake. This can go up to as much as 90 grams for active and athletic people.

Diet and nutritional supplementation can be tweaked to meet the requirement of necessary nutrients and ensuring adequate levels of neurotransmitters.

Neurotransmitters operate at different times of the day and are largely dictated by light exposure. Serotonin and dopamine are produced mostly during daylight hours. During dark hours the serotonin converts to melatonin and more GABA is present.

Brainwaves also correspond with the different neurotransmitters. In the morning the brain has alpha waves which increase to beta waves throughout the day. Gamma waves may also facilitate the daylight hours and ideal for higher learning. The alpha and beta waves facilitate acetylcholine and dopamine.

Other factors such as the seasons and a woman’s monthly menstrual cycle can also determine the amount of specific neurotransmitters needed and at which point of the cycle. For example in the luteal phase GABA is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter as this part of the phase we see a spike in the hormone progesterone which works synergistically with this calming neurotransmitter. Serotonin will also be present at this time as well as more conversion into melatonin to facilitate sleep.

Dopamine will be more available during the follicular phase which seems to increase with the rise of the female reproductive hormone estrogen.

In northern climates during winter with the lack of sunlight there is likely to be a lower production of serotonin or higher amounts of this may convert to melatonin possibly through daylight hours shortening. This may pick up slightly as spring emerges. Many people also find that spring makes them feel motivated.

Everyone is unique and a person’s life experience may dictate more of what neurotransmitter may be required on any given day. An example is a highly stressed person is very likely to be needing more serotonin and dopamine and possibly other neurotransmitters as the chronic stress quickly depletes all neurotransmitters and can lead to a state of depression.

Checking deficiency levels of neurotransmitters can help determine what may be needed along with using brainwave measuring device which uses electroencephalography (EEG) may help identify the neurotransmitters needed and balancing levels.

The difference in amounts of brain neurotransmitters found in men and women indicate that men need more dopamine than women and women need more serotonin than men.

Dopamine rises during the day and is low during the evening and in sleep. In women dopamine rises along with estradiol (estrogen) which rises in the follicular phase and peaks around ovulation.

Dopamine Supplementation

Nutritional co factors or agonists for dopamine are available in most supplemental forms. These include phenylalanine, tyrosine, iron, vitamin C, and vitamin B-complex as well as single individual B vitamins.

SAMe is also available as a supplement or methionine (cofactor amino acid for SAMe).

A healthy diet and where necessary supplements can maintain a healthy dopamine pathway.

Dopamine fasting which is a popular method in Silicon Valley to restore levels of dopamine by abstaining from activities that stimulate the dopaminergic pathway. Usually these activities are things that bring pleasure and may even become addictive. They include the excessive use of smartphones or other technological devices, social media, binge watching tv shows or movies, playing video games, excess food and sex. It is believed that through abstinence of pleasure activities dopamine levels may be restored.

Supplementing with a good quality omega 3 supplement will help assist with dopamine synthesis.

Magnesium and zinc levels should also be optimal as any imbalance will interfere with many bodily processes.

Reuptake inhibitors such as caffeine, nicotine and certain drugs are not the best way to increase neurotransmitters. Although caffeine and nicotine increase neurotransmitters such as dopamine they do this by maintaining the circulation of neurotransmitter which tricks the body into thinking more is available when in fact reserves are being used up. Optimal nutritional intake through diet and if required supplementation is the ideal way for neurotransmitter synthesis.

Supplements or nutrients that help raise dopamine levels are best taking in the morning to help levels rise throughout the daylight hours. Avoid taking supplements such as phenylalanine or tyrosine in the evening as it is too stimulating and may interfere with sleep.

    Tonic dopamine neurons release, fire spontaneously as sustained response and at low rates and is active for the duration of the stimulus.

    Phasic dopamine release is more associated with reward motivation than tonic dopamine release. [R] [R

    Although dopamine increases enthusiasm and motivation in a positive way it can also cause motivation to avoid experiences that cause aversion such as seen in war veterans when reminded of battle and associated combat stress. [R] [R]

    Dopamine receptor agonist mimic the actions of dopamine and may raise levels.

    Some serotonin receptors and subtypes impact levels of dopamine.

    Research is showing that 5-HT2A (serotonin subtype) antagonists may be useful in treatments for psychosis, alcohol and cocaine dependence which is likely because 5-HT2A antagonists interfere with the heightened state of dopamine activity associated with these conditions [R]

    5-HT2B (serotonin subtype) receptor is involved in stomach contraction and regulates cardiac structure and functions.

    5-HT2B antagonists have been used as a treatment for migraine headaches. [Rhttps://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27127104/]
